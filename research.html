Dissertation: Chance, Causation, Causal Loops
My dissertation aims to shed light on the nature of chance and causation. It does this by examining causal loops, hypothetical scenarios where a causal influence travels back in time and interacts with its source. Causal loops are curious structures. For example, in a world with causal loops, someone might escape from prison by traveling back in time after being freed and cause her own escape. Some have thought that causal loops are paradoxical or incoherent; however, I think that Lewis (1976, "Paradoxes of Time Travel") has convincingly established that causal loop scenarios, though curious, don't entail any genuine paradox or incoherence. Not only that, but spacetimes with causal loops have well-understood mathematical models, which have been subject to serious scientific inquiry. Accepting their possibility, we find in causal loops a powerful test case for our theories about chance and causation. Some of the central claims I defend are that chances on loops systematically deviate from ordinary chances; that chances aren't necessarily constant across intrinsically duplicate trials; that chance isn't as intimately tied to time or causation as is usually thought; and that causation is better reduced to spacetime structure and laws than to counterfactual dependence.
Published Papers:

  1. Immortal Beauty: Does Existence Confirm Reincarnation?
    2022. Australasian Journal of Philosophy. [abstract] [preprint] [link]
    • I argue that a popular view about self-locating evidence implies that there are cases in which agents have surprisingly strong evidence for their own reincarnation. The central case is an ‘Immortal Beauty' scenario, modelled after the well-known Sleeping Beauty puzzle. I argue that if the popular ‘thirder’ solution to the puzzle is correct, then Immortal Beauty should be confident that she's going to be reincarnated. The essay also examines another pro-reincarnation argument due to Michael Huemer (2021). I argue that his argument fails, and that my argument establishes an alternative way in which mere existence can be evidence for reincarnation. I then examine whether my result generalizes.
  2. List and Menzies on High-Level Causation
    2021. Pacific Philosophical Quarterly. [abstract] [preprint] [link]
    • I raise two objections against Christian List and Peter Menzies' influential account of high-level causation. Improving upon some of Stephen Yablo's earlier work, I develop an alternative theory which evades both objections. The discussion calls into question List and Menzies' main contention, namely, that the exclusion principle, applied to difference-making, is false.
